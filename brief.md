# What if you ate every iconic Mexican dish in one week?

## Style & Tone
Conversational second-person throughout — the viewer is the protagonist, never an observer. Tone is serious, cinematic, and aspirational: no jokes, no commentary, just declarative reporting of what happens. Sensory details (smell, sound, texture, heat) carry the emotional weight of every scene.

## Story Brief
You land in Mexico City with one goal: eat every iconic dish the country is known for, one day at a time. The arc moves fast — street tacos on day one, escalating through birria, tamales, elotes, mole, and mezcal, each stop pulling you deeper into a culture built around food. The pacing mirrors the country itself: chaotic, loud, overwhelming, then suddenly still and profound.

The obstacle arrives mid-week. You hit a taco stand you probably shouldn't have entered — the kind with no menu, no prices, and a cook who doesn't speak to customers. You eat something you can't identify. It stops you completely. Then you understand: this is the point. Not the food. The surrender.

By day seven, you've eaten your way from airport carnitas to a private kitchen in Oaxaca where a chef shows you a mole recipe that takes three days to make. You didn't come to Mexico to eat. You came to be changed by it.

## Key Points / Beats to Cover
- Opening hook — Day one. You land in Mexico City with an empty stomach and no plan. The smell of carnitas hits you before you leave the airport.
- Beat 1: Day 2 — You find a taco al pastor stand at 7am. Thirty people in line before the city wakes up. You eat four. You don't move for ten minutes.
- Beat 2: Day 3 — Birria in Guadalajara. The broth is dark red. You dip the taco and the cheese pulls a foot long. The cook watches you eat and says nothing.
- Beat 3: Day 5 — You enter a market stall in Oaxaca with no English menu. You point. You eat tlayudas, memelas, chapulines. One of them surprises you. You order it again.
- Climax: Day 6 — A questionable taco stand with no menu, no prices, no name. You eat something unidentifiable. It's the best thing all week. You don't ask what it was.
- Resolution / CTA: Day 7 — A private kitchen, a three-day mole, a chef who doesn't explain anything. You sit in silence after the last bite. You didn't eat every iconic Mexican dish. You ate the reason they exist.

## Additional Notes
Model this script on the following style rules from the analyzed channels:
- **Open with "Day one." and place the viewer directly inside the scenario** — no introduction, no context-setting, just action already in progress.
- **Use strict second-person "you" throughout** — the viewer is the protagonist, never an observer.
- **Structure the video as a time-lapse arc**: Day 1 → Day 2/3 → Week 1 → Month 6 → Year 1, with each interval escalating stakes or power.
- **Write in short declarative sentences of 8–15 words** — action verbs first, minimal adjectives, sensory details only (sound, smell, temperature, texture).
- **Include exactly one reversal or obstacle mid-arc** that the protagonist overcomes through skill or wit.
- **End with a single-sentence philosophical kicker** that reframes the entire story — never a subscribe ask or open question.
- **Keep the climax about dominance or transformation** — the protagonist doesn't just succeed, they are changed by the experience.
- **Title formula: "What if you [did X] in [place/setting]?"** — short, personal, always putting the viewer as the agent of change.

## Scene Structure
Each narrative beat (each "day") should span 2–3 scenes, not just one. The narration for a single day gets split across multiple consecutive clips — each clip carries 1–2 sentences that continue the same moment. This creates visual variety while keeping the narration dense and story-driven, like DD_Informer's continuous voice-over style. Do NOT compress an entire day into a single scene.
