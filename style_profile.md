### Hook Style
Videos open immediately with the title reframed as a direct "what if you" question, dropping the viewer straight into Day 1 of a scenario with zero preamble. The first sentence always establishes the premise and the second establishes an immediate sensory or situational detail: "Day one. You arrive in Greece with raw chicken, flour, oil, and a bronze pot." or "Day one, you wake up on your knees at the edge of the Nile. A crocodile waits in the water." The hook is never a question posed to the audience — it's a story already in motion.

### Pacing
Narration is extremely dense with very short time intervals — individual videos cover an entire arc across Days 1–7 or Years 1 in under 300 words, meaning each "day" label is a micro-scene of 1–3 sentences. The pacing is uniformly fast throughout: there is no slow middle section, each day builds on the last with a new escalation. The final scene often delivers a one-line philosophical kicker that reframes the whole story.

### Narration Length & Sentence Structure
Sentences are punchy and declarative — almost always 8–15 words. No em-dashes or ellipsis; instead, short separate sentences create rhythm: "You season the chicken and prepare it. Lower it into hot oil. The crackling sound cuts through conversation." Action verbs dominate; adjectives are sparse and sensory (hot, quiet, stunned, slow). The writing reads like a movie script treatment: only what you see and what happens.

### Storytelling Structure
The universal structure is a **time-lapse escalation arc**: Day 1 → Day 7 → Month 6 → Year 1, with each interval representing a new stage of rising success (or reversal). There is always one obstacle mid-arc (spoiled goods, a power structure pushing back, a rival emerging) that the protagonist solves, after which the final ascent to dominance or a reflective ending follows. The climax is always about total power or transformation: "You control all the power," "Your meat controls Egypt."

### Tone
Conversational second-person ("you") throughout — the viewer IS the protagonist in every video. There is no humour; the tone is serious, cinematic, and aspirational with flashes of tension. Emotional register sits between wonder and quiet triumph, occasionally punctuated by a single deadpan comic beat (tying up Socrates, calling him "a weirdo"). The narration never moralizes or editorializes — it reports events in a neutral, declarative voice and lets the power fantasy speak for itself.

### CTA Patterns
Videos end with a one-line summary statement that reframes the whole arc philosophically: "You didn't bring fried chicken to Greece. You brought them desire." / "The most dangerous thing you brought to ancient Rome wasn't stronger weapons. It was your brain." There is no explicit subscribe ask, no question posed to viewers, and no next-video tease. The ending is always a mic-drop aphorism that gives the viewer a takeaway to carry with them.

### Title Patterns
Titles use a consistent "What if you [did X] in [historical setting]?" formula, sometimes swapped to "Would you get rich doing X in Y?" or "[Modern X] VS [Historical Setting]". They are short (6–10 words), always feature a historical era, and frame the viewer as the agent of change. Numbers appear only as time markers within the video, not in titles.

Example titles:
1. What if you sold Fried Chicken in Ancient Greece?
2. Modern doctor VS medieval Europe
3. What if you brought electricity to ancient Egypt?
4. Weapon engineer VS Ancient Rome
5. What if you spent 1 week in ancient China?

### Visual Style Implications
The narration is built entirely around physical, tactile sensory details — sound, smell, texture — suggesting close-up cuts and atmosphere shots rather than sweeping vistas: "The crackling sound cuts through conversation. The smell spreads through warm Mediterranean air." Historical crowd reactions (soldiers going quiet, workers stopping, a court watching in stunned silence) imply frequent cut-to-crowd reaction shots. The day/week/month/year time jumps suggest title card transitions between distinct visual scenes.

### Key Takeaways for Scripting
- **Open with "Day one." and place the viewer directly inside the scenario** — no introduction, no context-setting, just action already in progress.
- **Use strict second-person "you" throughout** — the viewer is the protagonist, never an observer.
- **Structure the video as a time-lapse arc**: Day 1 → Day 2/3 → Week 1 → Month 6 → Year 1, with each interval escalating stakes or power.
- **Write in short declarative sentences of 8–15 words** — action verbs first, minimal adjectives, sensory details only (sound, smell, temperature, texture).
- **Include exactly one reversal or obstacle mid-arc** (spoilage, a rival power, an authority figure) that the protagonist overcomes through skill or wit.
- **End with a single-sentence philosophical kicker** that reframes the entire story — never a subscribe ask or open question.
- **Keep the climax about dominance or transformation** — the protagonist doesn't just succeed, they reshape the world: "You control all the power," "You didn't give Rome pizza."
- **Title formula: "What if you [did modern thing] in [ancient/historical setting]?"** — short, personal, always putting the viewer as the agent of change.
